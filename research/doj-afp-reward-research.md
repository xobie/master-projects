## 1. REWARD PROGRAMS

### 1.1 US Department of State — Rewards for Justice (RFJ)

**Authority:** 22 U.S.C. § 2708 (1984 Act to Combat International Terrorism)  
**Administered by:** Bureau of Diplomatic Security  
**Website:** [www.RewardsForJustice.net](https://rewardsforjustice.net)

#### What it covers:
- Information leading to arrest/conviction of anyone planning, committing, or aiding **international terrorist acts** against US persons or property
- **Disruption of financial mechanisms** of foreign terrorist organizations (including ISIS, Al-Shabaab, ISIS-Somalia)
- Identification of key terrorist leaders
- Disruption of **kidnapping networks** that financially support terror groups
- Foreign interference in US elections
- Malicious cyber activity directed by foreign governments
- North Korea sanctions evasion financial networks

#### Payout:
- Up to **$10 million** for information on key terrorist leaders / major networks
- Up to **$5 million** for specific attacks
- **Relocation assistance** may accompany rewards
- Recent active reward: **up to $10 million** for information on ISIS financial mechanisms including **ISIS-Somalia** (tweet dated August 18, 2025: *"Help us disrupt the money stream that funds ISIS violence. Share your information on ISIS-Somalia's financial mechanisms with us. Your tip could make you eligible for a reward and relocation."*)

#### How to submit:
- **Web form** at RewardsForJustice.net (most accessible)
- **Tor-based anonymous channel:** `he5dybnt7sr6cm32xt77pazmtm65flqy6irivtflruqfc5ep7eiodiad.onion`
- Multiple languages supported
- Provide: your name, location, preferred language, all supporting files (photos, videos, documents, financial records)
- An RFJ representative will contact you

#### Key distinction:
RFJ is explicitly targeting **international terrorism** and **foreign** terrorist organizations. ISIS-Somalia and ISIS-Iraq qualify. This is the most relevant US program for information about ISIS financing networks operating through Australia.

### 1.2 FBI — Tips & Leads

**Website:** [tips.fbi.gov](https://tips.fbi.gov)  
**Nature:** Intelligence-gathering, not a direct payout program per se  
**Coordination:** FBI tips feed into DOJ/RFJ reward processes when terrorism is involved

#### What it covers:
- Terrorism, terrorism financing, national security threats
- Financial crimes with federal nexus
- Money laundering, wire fraud, bank fraud

#### How to submit:
- Online: tips.fbi.gov (note: site blocks direct scraping but is publicly known)
- In person: any FBI field office
- Phone: FBI field offices listed at fbi.gov/contact-us/field-offices

#### Rewards:
- FBI itself doesn't run a stand-alone payout program; tips that result in prosecution may qualify for DOJ/RFJ rewards
- FBI coordinates with RFJ for international terrorism cases

### 1.3 US Treasury — OFAC (Office of Foreign Assets Control)

**Website:** [ofac.treasury.gov](https://ofac.treasury.gov)

#### What it covers:
- Sanctions violations — transactions with **Specially Designated Nationals (SDNs)**
- Entities owned 50%+ by SDNs are treated as blocked (OFAC 50 Percent Rule)
- Financing of designated terrorist organizations (ISIS/ISIL, Al-Shabaab, Al-Qaeda)

#### Reward mechanism:
- OFAC does **not** run its own cash reward program
- OFAC violations reported to OFAC → violations may result in **civil penalties to OFAC** → informants may be eligible for **FinCEN whistleblower rewards** or **DOJ rewards** depending on case trajectory
- **Critical:** If an Australian entity is funneling money to a designated SDN (e.g., ISIS), OFAC has jurisdiction over **US persons and US financial system touchpoints**. Australian banks that process USD transactions through US correspondent banks fall under OFAC reach.

#### FinCEN Whistleblower Program (31 U.S.C. § 5323):
- Established under the Anti-Money Laundering Act of 2020
- Awards: **10–30% of sanctions collected** exceeding $1 million threshold
- Covers: Bank Secrecy Act violations, AML violations, sanctions violations
- Submit via: [fincen.gov/submit-a-tip](https://www.fincen.gov/submit-a-tip)
- Protections: Anti-retaliation provisions in 31 U.S.C. § 5323

### 1.4 IRS Whistleblower Office

**Authority:** IRC § 7623  
**Website:** [irs.gov/whistleblower](https://www.irs.gov/compliance/whistleblower-office)

#### What it covers:
- Tax evasion, tax fraud
- Unreported income from illegal sources (which would include NDIS fraud proceeds)
- Violations of laws IRS is authorized to administer
- Proceeds from criminal fines, civil forfeiture, BSA reporting violations (post-2018 Bipartisan Budget Act)

#### Payout:
- **15–30% of collected proceeds** (mandatory award, not discretionary, for cases >$2 million in dispute)
- Below $2M threshold: discretionary award up to 15%
- As of FY2024: nearly $2 billion paid to ~400 whistleblowers since program modernization

#### How to submit:
- IRS Form 211 (Application for Award for Original Information)
- Must be specific, timely, credible, and original (not already public)
- Submit to: IRS Whistleblower Office, Stop 31313, Ogden, UT 84201

#### Australian nexus:
- If NDIS fraud proceeds are transferred to the US, or if any US person/entity is involved, IRS has potential jurisdiction
- Australian persons with US tax obligations (dual citizens, green card holders) committing fraud are especially relevant

### 1.5 SEC Whistleblower Program

**Authority:** Dodd-Frank Act  
**Website:** [sec.gov/whistleblower](https://www.sec.gov/enforcement-litigation/whistleblower-program)

#### What it covers:
- Securities law violations with US nexus
- Limited relevance for NDIS fraud unless publicly listed companies are involved
- **Possible nexus:** If a fraud scheme involves US-listed financial instruments, money market funds, or US-traded securities

#### Payout:
- **10–30% of sanctions collected** when sanctions exceed $1 million
- As of FY2023: nearly $2 billion paid to ~400 whistleblowers total

### 1.6 Australian Federal Police (AFP)

**Website:** [afp.gov.au](https://www.afp.gov.au)  
**Report a crime:** [afp.gov.au/report-crime](https://www.afp.gov.au/report-crime)

#### What it covers:
- Commonwealth crimes (crimes against the Australian government/Commonwealth)
- **Terrorism and terrorism financing** (Division 103, Criminal Code Act 1995)
- NDIS fraud (Commonwealth fraud)
- Grant fraud where Commonwealth funds are involved
- Money laundering

#### Key contact channels:
- **National Security Hotline:** 1800 123 400 (24/7, terrorism/suspicious behavior)
  - SMS: 0498 562 549
- **Crime Stoppers (anonymous):** 1800 333 000 or crimestoppers.com.au
- **AFP general report:** 131 AFP (131 237) or via afp.gov.au/report-crime
- **Airport Watch:** For suspicious activity at AFP-policed airports

#### Reward program:
- AFP does **not** operate a formal public cash reward program equivalent to RFJ
- Crime Stoppers Australia sometimes offers rewards for specific cases (funded by state/territory governments and sponsors)
- **However:** The AFP cooperates directly with US agencies (FBI, DEA, RFJ) under mutual assistance treaties (MLAT). Information provided to AFP that enables a US-linked prosecution may qualify for US reward programs.
- AFP Counterterrorism financial investigations can trigger referrals to RFJ if the terrorism has international character

#### Terrorism financing law (AFP jurisdiction):
Under **Division 103 of the Criminal Code Act 1995 (Cth)**:
- Illegal to **intentionally collect or provide money** knowing or being reckless about whether it will fund a terrorist act
- Covers both **direct attack costs** (travel, weapons) and **ongoing network funding** (recruiting, ideology promotion, living costs)
- AFP explicitly investigates: financing terrorism, High-Risk Terrorist Offenders (HRTO), terrorism-related kidnap for ransom
- Covers Australian fighters who participated in Iraq/Syria conflicts

### 1.7 AUSTRAC (Australian Transaction Reports and Analysis Centre)

**Website:** [austrac.gov.au](https://www.austrac.gov.au)

#### What it is:
Australia's **financial intelligence unit** and AML/CTF (Anti-Money Laundering / Counter-Terrorism Financing) **regulator**. AUSTRAC does not investigate crimes directly — it collects financial intelligence and shares it with AFP, ASIO, state police, and international partners.

#### AUSTRAC's relevance to tips:
- AUSTRAC receives **Suspicious Matter Reports (SMRs)** from financial institutions — not from members of the public directly
- A member of the public **cannot directly submit** an SMR to AUSTRAC
- **However:** Public tips with financial crime indicators should go to:
  1. **AFP** (via report-crime portal) — they work with AUSTRAC
  2. **Crime Stoppers** — passes tips to AFP/relevant agencies
  3. **NDIS Commission** — for NDIS-specific fraud

#### Key AUSTRAC intelligence products (publicly available):
- **Typologies and Case Studies Reports** — annual reports documenting known money laundering/terrorism financing patterns
- **Terrorism Financing Risk Assessment** (most recent: 2024)
- **Not-for-Profit sector risk guidance** — specifically covers risks of charity/grant funds being diverted to terrorism
- AUSTRAC has specifically flagged: remittance services to high-risk countries (Somalia, Iraq, Syria), cash-intensive businesses, and NFP sector as high TF risk

#### AUSTRAC's role in NDIS fraud context:
AUSTRAC would receive SMRs from banks about suspicious NDIS payment patterns. AUSTRAC passes financial intelligence to AFP for investigation. A public tip about NDIS fraud with terrorism financing suspicion should go to **AFP**, which then liaises with AUSTRAC.

## 2. KNOWN CASES — NDIS/Grant Fraud & Terrorism Financing Links

### 2.1 NDIS Fraud Scale (documented)

The NDIS is a massive target for organized fraud. Key documented facts:
- The scheme disburses ~$35–40 billion AUD annually (as of 2023–2024)
- The NDIS Fraud Taskforce (AFP + Services Australia) has prosecuted **hundreds of cases**
- Documented fraud typologies include:
  - **Phantom services** — billing for support that was never delivered
  - **Identity fraud** — fake participants and fake providers
  - **Provider collusion** — participants and providers splitting fraudulent payments
  - **Inflated billing** — charging for premium services when basic (or no) services delivered
  - **Organized crime involvement** — structured NDIS fraud rings operating across multiple providers

### 2.2 AFP Terrorism Financing Investigations (documented publicly)

Australia has had multiple documented terrorism financing prosecutions, many involving:
- **Somali diaspora remittance networks** — AUSTRAC has specifically flagged hawala and informal value transfer systems used by diaspora communities (Somali, Iraqi) as high-risk for terrorism financing
- **Iraqi community charity fraud** — ASIO and AFP have investigated cases where charities ostensibly serving Iraqi/Middle Eastern communities were suspected of diverting funds
- **Foreign fighters**: AFP has prosecuted Australians who funded travel to Syria/Iraq for ISIS. Some cases involved small amounts of money raised through legitimate-appearing community collections.

### 2.3 Intersection: NDIS/Grants + Terrorism Financing

**This is the crux of the hypothesis. Here is what is publicly documented vs. what remains speculative:**

#### Documented/publicly reported:
- AUSTRAC's **Typologies and Case Studies Reports** have documented cases where:
  - Disability support providers were used to launder proceeds of crime
  - NFP/charity structures were used to move funds offshore to high-risk jurisdictions
  - Remittance services used by community organizations (including in Somali and Iraqi communities) generated SMRs

- **Parliamentary Joint Committee on Intelligence and Security (PJCIS)** has received submissions raising concerns about:
  - Charities potentially linked to overseas extremist organizations
  - Community grant programs with inadequate vetting of recipients

- AFP annual reports have noted increasing complexity in terrorism financing, including legitimate-source funding (welfare, employment income) supplementing terrorist activity

#### What is NOT publicly documented (as of research date):
- A specific, named case of NDIS funds confirmed to have reached ISIS, Al-Shabaab, or similar designated terrorist organizations
- Confirmed government grant funds directly traced to terrorist financing in Australia

#### Important caveat:
Classified AFP/ASIO investigations may involve such cases — but by definition, these are not public. The absence of public cases does not mean no investigation exists. It means any tip you provide may be the first public piece of a classified picture.

### 2.4 RFJ Active Reward — ISIS-Somalia Financial Mechanisms

**This is directly relevant.** As of August 2025, Rewards for Justice was actively seeking:
> *"Share your information on ISIS-Somalia's financial mechanisms with us."*

This means the US government has an active, open reward offer for information about **how ISIS-Somalia finances itself globally**, including through diaspora networks, informal remittances, and potentially legitimate-appearing businesses or welfare/grant fraud in Western countries.

## 4. INVESTIGATION STRATEGY

This section outlines how to systematically find and document evidence that would qualify for a reward under the programs described in Section 1.

### 4.1 Qualifying Criteria — What Agencies Actually Need

For a tip to qualify for a reward (particularly RFJ), the information must be:
1. **Specific** — names, entities, transactions, amounts, dates, locations
2. **Credible** — corroborated by observable evidence, not just suspicion
3. **Original** — not already known to law enforcement or publicly reported
4. **Actionable** — leads directly to an arrest, conviction, or disruption of financial mechanisms

Generic suspicion ("I think NDIS fraud might fund terrorism") will not qualify. Documented evidence chains will.

### 4.2 Step-by-Step Investigation Framework

#### Phase 1: Build the Entity Map
1. Identify specific provider(s) or recipient organization(s) of interest
2. Look up ABN registration details (abn.business.gov.au)
3. Check ASIC for company structure, officeholders, related entities
4. Cross-reference NDIS Provider Register for registration status
5. Check grants.gov.au for any grant receipts to the same entities/ABNs
6. Check if any officeholders appear on OFAC SDN List or are known to AFP

#### Phase 2: Financial Pattern Analysis
1. Document what services the entity is registered to provide vs. what community members report they actually receive
2. Gather any invoices, payment records, or bank statements you have legitimate access to
3. Note any **structuring patterns** (multiple transactions just under $10,000 AUD threshold — AUSTRAC requires reporting of $10,000+ cash transactions)
4. Note any remittances to high-risk jurisdictions (Somalia, Iraq, Syria, Libya)
5. Look for shell entities — multiple ABNs with same officeholders or same registered address

#### Phase 3: Connect to Terrorism Financing Indicators
- Match entity names, phone numbers, email addresses, or ABNs against:
  - OFAC SDN List
  - UN Security Council 1267 Committee Consolidated List
  - Australian government's "listed terrorist organizations" (ag.gov.au)
- Look for evidence of funds leaving Australia to countries/individuals linked to terrorist organizations
- Evidence of individuals associated with the entity who have known extremist links (prior AFP prosecution, ASIO review, travel to conflict zones)

#### Phase 4: Documentation
- Keep a **chronological evidence log**: what you observed, when, where, with what supporting documents
- Preserve digital evidence: screenshots with timestamps, emails, financial records
- Note any witnesses (but do not pressure or alert potential subjects)
- **Do not confront** subjects or tip them off

#### Phase 5: Submission Strategy
1. **Primary submission**: AFP via afp.gov.au/report-crime — select "terrorism" or "fraud" as appropriate. Provide your full documented evidence package.
2. **Secondary submission**: RFJ (rewardsforjustice.net) if there is a clear ISIS/ISIL/Al-Shabaab financial mechanism link. This is where the cash reward potential is highest (up to $10 million).
3. **Parallel notification**: AFP National Security Hotline (1800 123 400) for immediate concern about terrorism financing
4. **FinCEN submission** (if US financial institutions are involved): fincen.gov/submit-a-tip

## 5. LEGAL CONSIDERATIONS

### 5.1 Australian Whistleblower Protections

#### Public Interest Disclosure Act 2013 (Cth) — Commonwealth public servants
- Protects Commonwealth government employees who disclose wrongdoing
- Administered by the Commonwealth Ombudsman (ombudsman.gov.au)
- Protections: immunity from civil/criminal liability, protection from reprisals

#### Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) — Private sector whistleblowers
- Protects employees of corporations reporting ASIC-regulated misconduct
- Protection applies if disclosure made to: ASIC, APRA, AFP, or a company's designated whistleblower officer

#### Limitations:
- Neither law provides blanket protection for any disclosure to any party
- Must follow prescribed channels (AFP, ASIO, AUSTRAC, relevant regulator)
- Disclosure must be in good faith based on reasonable belief — not malicious accusations

### 5.2 US Whistleblower Protections

#### FinCEN Whistleblower (31 U.S.C. § 5323):
- Anti-retaliation protection for employees who report BSA/AML violations
- Cannot be fired, demoted, or harassed for reporting

#### IRS Whistleblower (IRC § 7623):
- Civil action against employers for retaliation
- Reinstatement, back pay, attorney's fees

#### Dodd-Frank/SEC:
- Strongest protections — includes private cause of action if employer retaliates

### 5.3 Key Risks to Manage

#### Risk 1: Defamation
- Reporting to law enforcement with good faith belief is protected
- **Do not** publish allegations publicly (social media, media) without solid evidence — this creates defamation liability
- Submit only to authorized agencies

#### Risk 2: Tipping off subjects (Australia — Criminal Code Act 1995)
- It is a criminal offence in Australia to tip off a subject of an AFP investigation
- Once you report, do not tell the subject or anyone connected to them

#### Risk 3: Unauthorized access to information
- Do not hack, illegally access, or steal documents to build your case
- Evidence obtained illegally will be inadmissible and may expose you to criminal liability
- Only use information you have legitimate access to (employment access, personal communications, public records)

#### Risk 4: False or misleading statements to investigators
- Providing false information to the AFP or federal agencies is a criminal offence
- Only report what you have direct evidence of; clearly distinguish observation from inference

#### Risk 5: Personal safety
- If the persons involved have terrorism links, there is real personal risk
- Use anonymous reporting channels (Crime Stoppers, RFJ Tor channel) if safety is a concern
- Discuss personal protection with AFP if reporting in person

### 5.4 Before Taking Action — Recommended Steps

1. **Consult a lawyer** specializing in whistleblower law before making any formal report
2. In the US, **contact a whistleblower attorney first** (IRS/SEC awards are often claimed with attorney assistance; attorneys typically work on contingency)
3. Consider whether your employment contract has confidentiality clauses that might be triggered (though public interest disclosures typically override these)
4. Document your own position: employment history, access rights, when and how you came across the information

## 6. NEXT STEPS

### Immediate (within 1 week)
1. **Identify whether you have specific evidence** (names, transactions, amounts, dates) vs. general suspicion. This determines which path to take.
2. **Consult a lawyer** — in Australia, look for a solicitor specializing in whistleblower/public interest disclosure law. In the US, search the National Whistleblower Center (whistleblowers.org) for referrals.
3. **Cross-check entity names** against OFAC SDN List (free, searchable at ofac.treasury.gov/sanctions-list-search) and the Australian government's listed terrorist organizations list.

### Short-term (within 1 month)
4. **Build your evidence package**: entity map, financial pattern documentation, connection to terrorism indicators (if any)
5. **Submit to AFP** via the online crime reporting portal — specifically flag "terrorism financing" concern
6. **If ISIS financial mechanism**: simultaneously submit to RFJ (rewardsforjustice.net). The RFJ ISIS-Somalia reward is actively open.
7. **If US financial system touchpoint**: also submit to FinCEN

### Medium-term (1–3 months)
8. **Follow up with AFP**: keep your evidence log updated. AFP must acknowledge reports.
9. **Monitor OFAC and DOJ press releases** for any related enforcement actions — if your tip led to action, you may need to file an award claim within a specified window (e.g., 90 days for SEC claims after a Covered Action notice is posted)
10. If AFP does not act and you believe the evidence is strong, consider escalating to:
    - The NDIS Quality and Safeguards Commission
    - The Inspector-General of Intelligence and Security (IGIS)
    - Parliamentary Joint Committee on Intelligence and Security (via a public submission)
